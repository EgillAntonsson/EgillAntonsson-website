My workshop

I recently decided to do a short workshop about TDD at work
and for preparation I browsed the internet looking for a fitting assignment,
and I came across TDD in Unity - Part 1 by Charles Amat, where he sets up his assignment:
implementing the Life Gauge from Zelda: Breath of the Wild game.

I thought this assignment was fitting for the workshop,
thus I read the Life Gauge page on the Zelda wiki (which was linked in the 'Part 1' post),
and also played the game a bit, focusing on the Life Gauge behavior.
But I did not go through the 'TDD in Unity' series, as I was running out of preparation time.

My focus and task order in both my preparations and workshop walk-through:

  1. Write down the Life Gauge behavior from the user perspective
    (based on the wiki, and to a lesser degree my experience, as I'm not so good in Zelda: Breath of the Wild)
  2. Focus first on the (non visual) domain logic and implement all the requirements it should fulfill,
    as TDD can fully and easily be used in the domain model
  3. Afterwards the focus would shift to the visual GUI,
    where the value of TDD likely would dwindle the closer I got to the physical boundary of the screen,
    ending with just manually testing with my eyes.
    (Boundary layers section in Part 1 elaborates on 2. and 3.)

With this I held the workshop where I screen shared TDD-ing the implementation,
and me and the participants collaboratively completed some of the domain logic requirements,
when the bell rang and the time was up.

Comparing approaches of the assignment

After the workshop I was curious how Charles Amat solved it,
thus I watched on YouTube the whole TDD in Unity series.
I both enjoyed and learned from watching Charles Amat live coding and implementing the Life Gauge
and his end result solution is good and sound, and I thank Charles for his work and contribution.
but I noticed that his focus and task order quite differed from mine, as he:

  1. Starts planning how he would go about creating the heart container in Unity, from a GUI perspective
  2. Starts TDD-ing logic close to the GUI
  3. Ends with TDD-ing the domain logic, the Player Health in Part 7 and Part 8.
    The requirements on the domain logic are very simple
    and are far from covering the requirements of Breath of the Wild
    After re-reading the 'Part 1', I acknowledge that Charles does indicate
    that the domain logic requirements would be very simple, as he wrote:
    The Life Gauge depletes when damage is taken
    and replenishes when health regenerating items are consumed.
    Besides some animations and effects, that's all there is to it;
    the requirements for the Life Gauge are pretty straightforward.

TDD-ing the same assignment, but with my focus and task order

This is why I think it's worth doing a blog post series with the same assignment,
but with my focus and task order (see my ordered bulletin list above in My workshop section).

When focusing on the domain logic, there will mainly be .NET code and not much usage of the Unity editor
(though I run the tests via Unity Test Runner).
When the focus later goes to the GUI, then there will be more focus on Unity
(e.g some Unity GUI specific code, setting and configuring the GUI in the Unity editor).
The GUI implementation part will likely be similar to the TDD in Unity series,
thus I might decide to reference it heavily, when I get to it.

My format will also differ:
I will not live code it, but instead
I will write directly into the blog posts the RED / GREEN / REFACTOR cycles
and show the appropriate code for each step.
With this approach I filter away the smallest repetitive 'back and forth' steps of the cycles and in my reasoning,
usually skipping over my missteps, unless I want to show them to showcase the value and benefits of TDD.
Thus I will present a polished happy path (pretty much)
but demonstrating how the requirements and cycles guide and shape the code and my reasoning,
that will read like a soothing poetry of rhythmic red green refactor loops (at least that's my ambition).
There are pros and cons with each format, and I might later switch format depending on the task at hand.

The Life Gauge behavior from the user perspective

The below list is the behaviour summary from
the Life Gauge page on the Zelda Wiki for the Breath of the Wild game
(Small streamlining done for the sake of the assignment):

  • Definition and Start State
  • The Life Gauge measures Link's (the avatar) current amount of health
  • Health is visually represented in the form of Hearts, and they are in fractions of 4
  • The Life Gauge starts with 3 Hearts

  • Taking Damage and Dying
  • Whenever Link takes damage he will lose a certain amount of Heart fractions
    equal to the damage points taken (always losing at least 1 fraction)
  • When Link loses all of his Hearts the game ends
  • Link is protected from any damage that would instantly kill him as long as his Life Gauge is full
    and will instead be left alive with Hearth 1 fraction remaining
    • However, if the damage is more than five Hearts above his full Life Gauge, he will still die

  • Replenishing and Maximum
  • Link's Life Gauge can be replenished by:
    • Picking up recovery Hearts / drinking recovery Potions
    • Resting while Sitting or Sleeping.
  • The Life Gauge can be extended to a maximum of 30 Hearts
  • It can also be temporarily extended by consuming certain Foods that grant yellow temporary Hearts
    • These yellow Hearts are lost when Link takes damage and cannot be recovered

Domain / Visual SoC mapping of Life Gauge

Domain Model GUI / View
Avatar Health Heart Container
- Heart (contains 4 Fractions)
Health Points Heart Fraction

This Separation of Concerns (SoC) mapping is helpful and good to establish early:
Heart Container (with its Hearts and fractions) is a specific GUI representation
of the Avatar Health, the domain model.

In a real-life scenario things are likely to change along the way,
e.g. the team might decide to change the GUI to a health bar or something else.

I'll apply the approach noted in the Boundary layers section in Part 1).
Make sure that the code dependency is minimal and one-way:
The view can depend in some way on the model, but not vice-versa.
And put all the domain logic into the model and keep the view thin and humble.

When future change requests on the GUI arrive, it will be effortless to handle as:

  • The domain and its logic will be unaffected (as it does not depend on the view)
  • The model naming and terms will be unaffected (as it does not depend on the view)

I might decide to have more than one GUI representations simultaneously to fully illustrate this.
It is quite common in game UI to have more than one GUI representation of the same model object.
